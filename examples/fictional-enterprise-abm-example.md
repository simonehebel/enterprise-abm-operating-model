# Fictional Enterprise ABM Example

## Important Note

This is a fictional example created solely to demonstrate how the frameworks and templates in this repository can be applied.

The company, accounts, market data, opportunity values and recommendations are fictional.

No confidential employer, customer, account or proprietary information is included.

---

## 1. Company Context

**Company:** Nexora Systems

**Business model:** Enterprise B2B SaaS

**Solution:** Supply chain risk and operational resilience platform

**Primary region:** DACH

**Target industries:**

- manufacturing,
- automotive,
- industrial technology,
- logistics.

**Average Sales cycle:** 9 to 15 months

**Primary business objective:** Build and accelerate enterprise pipeline within strategically relevant DACH accounts.

Nexora Systems helps large organizations identify supply chain risks, improve supplier transparency and strengthen operational resilience.

---

## 2. Business Situation

Nexora Systems has a strong product and several international customer references.

However, its DACH go-to-market execution is fragmented.

Current challenges include:

- a broad target-account list without clear prioritization,
- inconsistent Sales and Marketing coordination,
- limited differentiation between strategic and scalable accounts,
- generic messaging across industries and buying roles,
- weak visibility into buying committee engagement,
- inconsistent Sales follow-up,
- limited measurement of account progression.

The organization needs a structured ABM operating model that concentrates resources on accounts with the strongest strategic fit, customer need and commercial potential.

---

## 3. ABM Business Objective

The primary objective is to create and accelerate qualified enterprise pipeline.

Supporting objectives include:

- increase engagement within strategic accounts,
- improve access to executive and operational buying roles,
- strengthen Sales and Marketing alignment,
- improve opportunity progression,
- create a repeatable account-review model,
- identify which accounts justify additional investment.

### Expected Business Outcome

Within two quarters, the program should:

- increase meaningful engagement in priority accounts,
- improve buying committee coverage,
- create qualified enterprise meetings,
- support opportunity creation,
- accelerate selected active opportunities,
- improve visibility into account and pipeline progression.

---

## 4. Ideal Account Profile

The ideal account profile includes organizations with:

- more than 2,000 employees,
- complex supplier networks,
- multiple production or logistics locations,
- significant operational or regulatory risk,
- active supply chain transformation initiatives,
- executive pressure to improve resilience,
- sufficient budget and organizational readiness,
- an established regional Sales owner.

### Priority Signals

Priority accounts may show signals such as:

- public supply chain transformation programs,
- new regulatory requirements,
- executive appointments in procurement or operations,
- investments in supplier risk management,
- expansion into new markets,
- increased supplier complexity,
- active digital transformation initiatives,
- engagement with relevant content or events.

---

## 5. Account Selection Criteria

Each account is assessed across six dimensions.

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| Strategic Fit | Alignment with priority industries, regions and company strategy |
| Commercial Potential | Estimated deal size, expansion potential and long-term value |
| Customer Need | Strength and urgency of the business problem |
| Relationship Strength | Existing access, credibility and Sales relationships |
| Buying Readiness | Evidence of active interest, timing or transformation activity |
| Execution Feasibility | Sales ownership, data, content, budget and organizational capacity |

Scores range from 1 to 5.

## 7. Account Tiering Decision

### Tier 1: Strategic Account

**Account:** Alpha Manufacturing Group

**Recommended ABM approach:** 1:1 ABM

Rationale:

- highest strategic relevance,
- strong commercial potential,
- significant supply chain complexity,
- existing Sales relationship,
- active transformation initiative,
- confirmed Sales commitment,
- sufficient evidence to justify personalized investment.

Recommended investment:

- detailed account research,
- account-specific value proposition,
- buying committee mapping,
- executive engagement,
- personalized content,
- tailored workshop,
- continuous Sales and Marketing coordination.

### Tier 2: Priority Account Cluster

**Accounts:**

- Beta Mobility Systems
- Gamma Industrial Technologies

**Recommended ABM approach:** 1:Few ABM

Rationale:

- similar industry challenges,
- comparable operational resilience priorities,
- shared regulatory and supply chain pressures,
- sufficient commercial potential,
- opportunity for industry-specific activation.

Recommended investment:

- automotive and industrial resilience campaign,
- executive roundtable,
- industry-specific content,
- targeted nurture,
- coordinated Sales outreach,
- monthly account reviews.

### Tier 3: Scalable Target Accounts

**Accounts:**

- Delta Logistics Network
- Epsilon Components

**Recommended ABM approach:** 1:Many ABM

Rationale:

- relevant account fit,
- moderate customer need,
- limited current relationship,
- early buying readiness,
- insufficient evidence for highly personalized investment.

Recommended investment:

- targeted digital campaigns,
- segmented content,
- lifecycle nurture,
- intent monitoring,
- engagement scoring,
- trigger-based Sales follow-up.

### Nurture or Deprioritize

**Account:** Zeta Consumer Products

Recommended decision:

- do not include in the active ABM program,
- maintain limited nurture,
- reassess only if stronger buying signals or strategic relevance emerge.

## 8. Tier 1 Account Strategy

### Account

Alpha Manufacturing Group

### Strategic Rationale

Alpha Manufacturing Group operates across multiple production locations and has a complex international supplier network.

Public information indicates that operational resilience and supplier transparency are current strategic priorities.

The existing Sales relationship and potential commercial value justify a focused 1:1 ABM approach.

### Primary Customer Problem

The organization lacks sufficient visibility into supplier risk and operational dependencies across regions.

### Business Impact

Potential consequences include:

- production disruption,
- supplier concentration risk,
- increased operational cost,
- compliance exposure,
- delayed management decisions,
- limited resilience planning.

### Cost of Inaction

Without improved supplier transparency, the organization may continue reacting to supply chain disruptions instead of identifying and mitigating risks earlier.

## 11. Messaging by Buying Role

### Chief Operating Officer

**Priority problem:** Limited visibility into operational dependencies

**Core message:** Identify supply chain vulnerabilities before they become operational disruptions.

**Desired action:** Executive briefing

### Chief Procurement Officer

**Priority problem:** Fragmented supplier risk information

**Core message:** Create a structured and transparent view of supplier risk across categories and regions.

**Desired action:** Supplier risk workshop

### Chief Information Officer

**Priority problem:** Additional technology complexity

**Core message:** Integrate risk insight into the existing enterprise technology environment without creating an isolated system.

**Desired action:** Technical validation session

### Supply Chain Director

**Priority problem:** Difficulty translating risk data into action

**Core message:** Prioritize operational risks and coordinate mitigation across functions.

**Desired action:** Use-case demonstration

### Risk and Compliance Lead

**Priority problem:** Inconsistent governance and documentation

**Core message:** Improve supplier risk governance, documentation and executive transparency.

**Desired action:** Governance discussion

## 12. Engagement Strategy

### Phase 1: Insight and Preparation

Activities:

- validate account research,
- review the current opportunity,
- map the buying committee,
- identify relationship gaps,
- validate customer priorities with Sales,
- develop the account-specific value proposition.

### Phase 2: Executive Relevance

Activities:

- send an executive insight brief,
- invite the Chief Operating Officer to a resilience roundtable,
- share relevant industry evidence,
- coordinate personalized Sales outreach.

### Phase 3: Operational Engagement

Activities:

- conduct a supplier risk workshop,
- demonstrate relevant use cases,
- involve procurement and supply chain stakeholders,
- identify operational priorities and barriers.

### Phase 4: Technical and Commercial Validation

Activities:

- conduct an integration discussion,
- provide security and governance documentation,
- validate implementation requirements,
- support procurement preparation.

### Phase 5: Opportunity Progression

Activities:

- confirm the business case,
- document stakeholder alignment,
- agree the next commercial milestone,
- review risks and required executive support.

## 15. Governance Cadence

### Weekly Tier 1 Review

Participants:

- account executive,
- ABM or Field Marketing owner,
- Sales leader when required,
- solution or product stakeholder when required.

Agenda:

1. New account insight
2. Buying committee changes
3. Recent engagement
4. Sales follow-up
5. Opportunity progression
6. Barriers and risks
7. Next-best actions
8. Owners and deadlines

### Monthly ABM Program Review

Topics:

- performance across Tier 1 and Tier 2 accounts,
- engagement progression,
- buying committee coverage,
- Sales response,
- content performance,
- opportunity creation,
- budget and resource needs.

### Quarterly Business Review

Topics:

- account tier movement,
- pipeline contribution,
- strategic account additions or removals,
- program ROI,
- operating model gaps,
- leadership decisions.

## 18. Fictional 90-Day Plan

### First 30 Days

1. Validate account scoring and tiering.
2. Confirm Sales and Marketing ownership.
3. Complete Tier 1 account research.
4. Map the buying committee.
5. Develop the value proposition.
6. Agree shared KPIs and follow-up standards.

### Days 31–60

1. Launch personalized Tier 1 engagement.
2. Activate the Tier 2 industry campaign.
3. Conduct the first executive roundtable.
4. Start account engagement reporting.
5. Review Sales follow-up.
6. Identify buying committee gaps.

### Days 61–90

1. Evaluate account progression.
2. Adjust messaging and content.
3. Review Tier 1 investment.
4. Decide whether Tier 2 accounts should move tiers.
5. Return inactive accounts to nurture.
6. Present results and recommendations to leadership.

## 19. Risks and Mitigation

| Risk | Business Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Weak Sales commitment | Engagement does not progress | Confirm ownership before activation |
| Generic messaging | Low account relevance | Adapt messaging by account and buying role |
| Missing proof points | Reduced credibility | Prioritize references and outcome evidence |
| Poor account data | Inaccurate targeting | Validate account and stakeholder information |
| Too many Tier 1 accounts | Resources become diluted | Limit personalized investment |
| Missed follow-up | Account interest is lost | Define response times and escalation |
| Limited executive access | Weak strategic engagement | Use executive sponsors and partner relationships |

## Key Learning

Enterprise ABM should not be treated as a campaign targeted at a list of large companies.

A credible ABM operating model connects:

- business priorities,
- account selection,
- customer need,
- account tiering,
- buying committee relevance,
- Sales and Marketing ownership,
- engagement strategy,
- governance,
- pipeline progression,
- commercial outcomes.
